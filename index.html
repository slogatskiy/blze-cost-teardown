What actually drives Backblaze's cost of storage

A HDD-based cloud storage business whose largest cost input is the depreciation of finance-leased hard drives — not cash drive purchases. Here is how the cost base is built, how sensitive margins are to drive prices, and why the "5-year Neocloud deal" cuts the opposite way from how it first reads.

The reframe

Backblaze's headline multi-year contracts — the $335M, five-year CoreWeave deal and the B2 "Neo Cloud" deal — are contracts where Backblaze is the storage supplier, not the customer. It does not sign 5-year deals that lock in its own drive costs. On the cost side it is short/floating, and today's ~30% equipment inflation passes through with only its existing lease vintages as a buffer.

Cost inputs — depreciation, not drive purchases

Backblaze does not expense drives when it buys them. It capitalizes "hundreds of thousands of hard drives" via finance leases and recognizes the cost as depreciation over ~6 years. So the P&L cost of storage is dominated by D&A, and the drive-price risk hides inside it.

HDD vs SSD — the cost moat, and where inflation lands

B2 Cloud Storage runs on HDDs (Toshiba, Western Digital), not SSDs. HDD is ~$10–20/TB vs ~$79/TB for enterprise SSD — but the AI build-out is buying the same nearline HDDs, so the multiple has exploded and drive costs are climbing.

Contracts & inflation exposure

The PM's question — "do the 5-year Neocloud deals account for cost inflation?" — inverts once you see which side of the table Backblaze sits on. The multi-year deals are revenue. The cost side is a separate, floating exposure.

▲ Revenue side — Backblaze SELLS storage

Multi-year deals = provider contracts

$335MCoreWeave — 5-year, multi-exabyte HDD storage for AI object-store (Jun 2026). Plus 4.19M warrants at $7.60.
>$15MB2 "Neo Cloud" — eight-figure TCV, 2-year (not 5), revenue starting 2027.

Backblaze is monetizing the AI storage surge as a low-cost supplier. Risk: it commits to supply at contracted economics for 5 years while its own HDD input inflates.

▼ Cost side — Backblaze BUYS drives

Financing is short & floating

2–6 yrDrives & equipment on finance leases (~12.3% discount rate). No long-dated fixed-price drive supply that hedges inflation.
$4.7MFY2025 cash capex — vs $18.2M principal lease payments. Drives are financed, not bought outright.

June 2026 credit amendment expanded permitted capital-lease debt — leaning further into leasing as costs rise. The ~30% inflation passes through, buffered only by existing lease vintages.

05

One flag to normalize before you model

The April 2025 useful-life extension flatters reported margin

Effective 1 Apr 2025, Backblaze extended depreciable lives of data-center equipment (incl. hard drives) and servers from 3–5 years to a uniform 6 years.

  • Cut FY2025 depreciation ~$5.2M and added ~$0.09 to EPS.
  • Lifted FY2025 gross margin ~4pts (to 61% from 54%) — most of the year-over-year "improvement."
  • This is an accounting-estimate change, not a real cost reduction. It front-loaded a tailwind that reverses into a 2026 headwind.
  • Implies a ~6-year drive replacement cycle — normalize underlying unit economics for it.
06

Verdict — does BLZE benefit or get hurt?

It cuts both ways, exactly as the PM intuited — but the two directions hit different lines of the P&L.

▼ COSTS UP — today's regime

Net negative on unit cost

Higher lease liabilities, interest and depreciation. Management guides FY2026 gross margin down a few hundred bps; capex jumps to a mid-thirties % of revenue. Buffered — but not hedged — by 6-yr depreciation smoothing and existing lease vintages.

▲ COSTS DOWN — chip deflation

Clean tailwind

Cheaper drive vintages lower future depreciation and lift GAAP margin over the replacement cycle. This is the "works both ways" upside — and Backblaze's operating leverage means it flows to the bottom line.

The net question

The same HDD tightness that raises Backblaze's costs also drives customers to its cheap HDD tier (CoreWeave, Neo Cloud). The bull case is that incremental high-margin provider revenue out-earns the cost inflation. That netting isn't yet visible in disclosure — confirm the CoreWeave deal's realized margin, drive-fill economics, and warrant dilution before underwriting it.
